What is a Climograph?

A climograph (also known as a climatic diagram) is a single graphical chart that displays the basic climate of a specific location. It serves as a powerful tool for geographers, meteorologists, and students to visualize the long-term weather patterns of a region, specifically focusing on two key variables: temperature and precipitation.

Unlike a standard weather forecast that tells you what will happen tomorrow, a climograph represents averages usually collected over a period of 30 years. This data helps define the "climate personality" of a city, determining whether it belongs to a tropical rainforest, a desert, or a tundra biome.

An example of a Mediterranean climate: Wet winters (blue bars) and dry, hot summers (red line).

How to Read a Climograph

Reading these charts requires looking at three distinct axes simultaneously. Here is the breakdown of the visual components:

  • The Bottom Axis (X-Axis): This represents the 12 months of the year, usually starting with January on the left and ending with December on the right. This allows you to track seasonal changes.
  • The Bar Graph (Blue): The vertical bars represent the average monthly precipitation (rainfall or snowfall). You read the height of these bars against the axis on one side (often the left), usually measured in millimeters (mm) or inches.
  • The Line Graph (Red): The continuous line hovering over the bars represents the average monthly temperature. You read the points on this line against the axis on the opposite side, measured in degrees Celsius (°C) or Fahrenheit (°F).

Why Are They Important?

Climographs are essential for understanding the relationship between climate and human activity. For agriculture, knowing the "growing season" (the warm months) and the "wet season" is vital for planting crops. Farmers in a Monsoon climate, for example, rely on the heavy rains shown on the chart during summer months.

They are also crucial for determining Global Climate Classification. The shape of the line and bars allows scientists to categorize a region using the Köppen system. A straight, flat temperature line typically indicates a tropical location near the equator, while a steep curve (like a bell shape) suggests a location in the Northern Hemisphere with hot summers and cold winters. Conversely, a "U-shaped" temperature line indicates a location in the Southern Hemisphere, where June and July are the coldest months of the year.

By mastering the climograph, you aren't just looking at data; you are visualizing the rhythm of life in different corners of the Earth.

What's Climograph Guesser?

Climograph Guesser is a unique educational and competitive tool designed to test your climate analysis skills. It challenges you to interpret a presented climograph and accurately pinpoint its location on a world map.

Unlike a simple guessing game, scoring is based on a rigorous quantitative method. This makes it highly suitable for competitive play, use in classrooms, or for deepening personal understanding of global climate patterns.

How the Game Works

  • Goal: To place a pin on the world map at the location whose actual climate best matches the presented climograph.
  • Scoring: Your score is calculated based on the similarity between the climate data of the location you pin and the data shown in the target climograph.

The Scoring Mechanism (Quantitative Approach)

The Climograph Guesser system uses advanced similarity calculations to objectively determine which two locations on Earth have the closest climate. The system treats the two key climate variables as 12-dimensional vectors.

1. Climate Data Vectorization

The monthly average data for 12 months is represented by two distinct vectors:

  • Temperature Vector $\vec{T}$: The average temperature data for each month (Jan-Dec). $$\vec{T} = (T_{Jan}, T_{Feb}, T_{Mar}, \dots, T_{Dec})$$
  • Precipitation Vector $\vec{P}$: The average precipitation data for each month (Jan-Dec). $$\vec{P} = (P_{Jan}, P_{Feb}, P_{Mar}, \dots, P_{Dec})$$

2. Similarity Calculation (Cosine Similarity)

We use Cosine Similarity $S(\vec{A}, \vec{B})$ to determine how closely the climate data pattern of the location you pin ($A$) aligns with the pattern of the target climograph ($B$). This measure is robust because it focuses on the pattern rather than the magnitude.

$$S(\vec{A}, \vec{B}) = \frac{\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B}}{\|\vec{A}\| \|\vec{B}\|}$$

This calculation is performed independently for both the temperature and precipitation vectors. This method is exceptionally superior from a metrological perspective because it correctly handles regions with very low precipitation, such as deserts, where a slight measurement error could lead to a large absolute difference. Cosine similarity ensures the "pattern" (e.g., near-zero precipitation all year) is accurately assessed.

The final score is a weighted integration of these temperature and precipitation similarities, normalized to a maximum of 100 points.
